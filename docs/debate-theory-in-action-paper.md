### 3. Alternative Framework: Critics Should Put Theory Into Action and Consider All Considerations

> "Ignoring the policy implications of theory is often a sign of intellectual laziness on the part of the theorist. It is hard work to learn about the policy world and to make the connections from theory to policy. […] Often, when the policy implications are considered, flaws in the theory (or at least in the presentation of the theory) are uncovered. Thus, focusing attention on policy implications should lead to better theorizing." (Feaver, 2001, p. 178).
> "Bringing policy relevance back in thus does not mean driving theory out. […] Greater pride of place needs to be given to research questions defined in policy terms […]intellectuals cannot go on forever avoiding their share of responsibility for the world and hiding their distaste for politics under an alleged need to be independent. It is easy to have independence in your program and then leave others to carry that program out. If everyone thought that way, pretty soon no one would be independent." (Jentleson, 2002).

It is lazy ignorance to tell others to rethink how to stop nuclear war, while keeping one's hand clean of any problematic system. The best negative strategy is therefore a specific counterplan or a general alternative approach that addresses the advantage's harm. Specific solvency turns are even better than far-out net benefits, and they may carry a philosophic net benefit—such as reforming schools in a denormalized or anti-capitalist way. Modern debate began with the 1947 NDT organized by West Point, and the first few topics were about capitalism vs. communism, but centered around specifics like labor unions, price controls, and trade. Just because the affirmative omitted critical concerns does not mean those authors would object to the harm being addressed; they would appreciate shaping the policy or public understanding. Many critics should have always been literature base credibility indicts or general solvency turns, or advantage takeouts, or "reject government action as a general principle" which is categorizable as a Kantian ethic, even if the harm is ongoing. They are not in a magic circle as if we only have to win this to ignore all else.

Alternative thought systems to solve a harm can be used when the case is not wholly conceded, but is instead answered with how the plan could make things worse, alongside impact defense showing the harm is a minor drop in the bucket. For example, the negative would need to show that, instead of closing Guantánamo Bay, one should first rethink from the commitment to uphold our ethic to stop torture—including how to reclassify the Chinese Uyghurs and similar groups so that they are not tortured by their home country.
The critique alternative should specify the framework and the agent it is calling on. Martin notes the default that obtains when this specification is absent:

> "Without detailed ideas of methods and alternatives, most people will rely on the models with which they are most familiar, such as existing large-scale bureaucracies, decisions by elites and advice from experts" (Martin, 1990).
> "The voting public, the public which must be won over if the Left is to emerge from the academy into the public square, sensibly wants to be told the details…. [we] should not let the abstractly described best be the enemy of the better. We should not let speculation about a totally changed system, and a totally different way of thinking about human life and human affairs, replace step-by-step reform of the system we presently have" (Rorty, 1998, pp. 102–105).

## Conclusion

The paper argues that critical frameworks fail their burden when they reject specific policies for not dismantling capitalism, securitization, or governmentality. This commits the Nirvana and causal reductionism fallacies by comparing real proposals to idealized alternatives instead of feasible opportunity costs. Most cited critical authors are reformers, not advocates of categorical rejection. Treating their work as preclusive misreads them and violates equal epistemic standing. Critical concerns belong as solvency turns, credibility indicts, or permutations—not gateway issues that excuse inaction. Rethinking and action are one process. Always vote for the permutation so we can survive together. Pluralist synergy beats purity politics. Letting one claim preclude all else teaches prejudice, which leads to the extremist political mindsets that risk collective survival.

In contrast, this paper calls for a pluralist common cause via a reasoning environment for research, news tracking, and public argument. Its seven elements — AI-assisted debate, collective thought, transparency, simulation, live news outlines, post-self alignment, and inter-subjective consensus on TRUTH — describe a system in which debate becomes both a method of learning and a model for accountable AI reasoning. Such a platform enables citizens of the world to read the news not as noise, but as a navigable outline of competing claims, warrants, and futures.
